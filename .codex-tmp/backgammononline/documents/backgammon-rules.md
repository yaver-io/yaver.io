# The Rules of Backgammon

*Based on "The Rules of Backgammon" by Phil Simborg (Wycliffe Brothers).*

## 1. What is Backgammon?
Backgammon is considered to be the world’s oldest board game - over 5,000 years old. It is a combination of skill and luck, but tournaments played all over the world prove conclusively that the more skill you have, the more you will win. Backgammon is essentially an obstacle race between two armies. The first player to get all his checkers around the board and then off the board is the winner.

## 2. Basic Rules of Backgammon
Each player starts out in a set position with 15 checkers. The object of the game is to move your checkers around the board and take them all off before your opponent does. Bearing off your checkers can only begin once all your checkers are safely in your home board.

### A. Opening Roll
At the beginning of the game, each player rolls one die. The player with the higher roll plays the combination of the two dice. For example, if White rolls a 4 and Black rolls a 2, White gets to play a 4-2. The turn ends when White completes his move and picks up his dice. Then, Black gets to roll 2 dice and can play his roll. If doubles are rolled on the opening roll, both players simply roll again.

### B. Checker Movement
Rolling the dice determines how you are allowed to move your checkers. 
- If you roll a 3 and a 4, you may move one checker 3 "pips" or 3 points, and another checker 4 pips; or you may move the same checker 3 and then 4 or 4 and then 3. Note that the center bar is not counted when moving the checkers.
- A player may not land on a point that is occupied by 2 or more of the opponent’s checkers. 
- If a player lands on a point occupied by only one of his opponent’s checker, that checker is "hit" and must be placed on the center bar and starts all over.
- Once a checker is on the bar, it must enter into the opposing player’s "home board" before any other checkers can be moved. A player may not move any checkers on the board while there is still one or more checkers on the bar.
- If a player rolls the dice and there is a legal move that can be made, it must be made, and if only one checker can be legally moved, the higher number on the dice must be played. If it is not possible to move or re-enter from the bar, the turn is forfeited.

### C. Doubles
When doubles are rolled, the move is doubled. Therefore, if you roll a 5-5 you get to move 4 times (4 x 5 = 20 pips in total). You can move one checker all four times, or 4 different checkers, or any combination of checkers.

### D. Bearing Off
You cannot remove checkers from the board until all of your checkers are in your home board. Once they are in your home board, you may remove checkers as dictated by the dice.
- If you roll a 3 and you have one or more checkers on the 3 point, you may take a checker off from the 3 point. 
- You could also move a checker 3 pips from a higher point (e.g. from point 6 to point 3).
- If you do not have any checkers on the point rolled and no checkers on higher points, then you must move a checker from the highest occupied point.

### E. Single, Gammon, Backgammon
- **Single (1 point):** The player who gets all of his checkers off first wins 1 point.
- **Gammon (2 points):** If a player gets all of his checkers off before his opponent gets *any* checkers off, the win is doubled to 2 points.
- **Backgammon (3 points):** If a player gets all of his checkers off before his opponent gets any checkers off, AND there is one or more of the opponent’s checkers either in the player’s home board or still on the bar, the point is tripled to 3 points.

## 3. The Doubling Cube
At the beginning of the game, the doubling cube is placed halfway between the 2 players with 64 facing up. Every game begins at a stake of 1 point per game. The doubling cube is used to raise the stakes.
- Either player, before his roll only, may decide to raise the stakes by offering the cube on 2 to his opponent.
- The player who is doubled may accept the cube and put it on his side of the board (now the game is played for 2 points).
- If the player rejects or "drops" the cube, the game is over and he loses 1 point.
- If accepted, the player with the cube has the option of "redoubling" later.

## 4. Basic Strategy
- The basic goal is to move your checkers around the board safely while making it difficult for your opponent. 
- **Making Points:** A point is made when two or more checkers occupy the point. Try to make your 5 and 7 points early.
- **Primes:** If you can get 6 points in a row, that is called a 6-Prime, and it is impossible for your opponent to get by.
- **Hitting:** In the early game, it is generally a good idea to "hit" your opponent’s single checkers to put them further behind in the "race".

### Match Play
In tournaments, Match Play is used where players compete to a specified odd number of points (e.g., 5, 7, or 9).
- **Crawford Rule:** When one player reaches a score that is one point short of the match, neither player can use the doubling cube in the subsequent game (the Crawford game).
- **Jacoby Rule (Single Game/Money Play only):** If neither player has offered the doubling cube during the course of the game, gammons and backgammons count only as a single game.
